Composting for small urban balconies

Introduction

Composting doesn't require a big backyard. With the right container and a bit of routine, a balcony corner can turn kitchen scraps into rich, usable soil within a few months.

This guide walks through the essentials: what to buy, what to feed your bin, and how to keep the whole system pleasant to live next to.

Choosing containers

Look for a sealed tumbler or a stackable worm bin — both handle small spaces well and limit pests. Avoid open piles on a balcony; they invite flies and neighbors' complaints alike.

A two-bin rotation lets one batch finish curing while you keep adding scraps to the other.

Greens and browns

Balance nitrogen-rich "greens" (vegetable scraps, coffee grounds) with carbon-rich "browns" (dry leaves, shredded cardboard) at roughly a 1:3 ratio by volume.

Too many greens and the pile turns slimy and smelly; too many browns and it stalls out and stops breaking down.

Managing moisture

Aim for the texture of a wrung-out sponge. If it's soggy, mix in more dry browns. If it's crumbly and dry, add a light misting of water.

Troubleshooting odors

A sour or ammonia smell usually means too much moisture or too many greens. Turn the pile, add dry material, and give it airflow.

Harvesting compost

After 6 to 10 weeks, finished compost looks dark, crumbly, and smells like earth. Sift out any large fragments and return them to the bin for another round.
