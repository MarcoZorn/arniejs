Composting for Small Urban Gardens

Turning kitchen scraps into rich soil doesn't require acres of land. A tight rotation, the right ratio of greens to browns, and a little patience is all it takes to build a compost system that keeps pace with a household of four.

Start with a bin no bigger than a barrel. Layer your nitrogen-rich scraps — vegetable peels, coffee grounds, eggshells — with carbon-rich material like dry leaves or shredded cardboard. Turn it weekly and keep it as damp as a wrung-out sponge.

Within eight to twelve weeks you'll have a dark, crumbly amendment ready to feed next season's beds. The smell should be earthy, never sour — if it turns sharp, add more browns and turn it more often.

Worm bins are a faster alternative for apartment dwellers. A pound of red wigglers can process half a pound of scraps a day, and the castings they leave behind are some of the most potent fertilizer you can make at home.

Whatever method you choose, the goal is the same: close the loop between what your kitchen throws away and what your garden needs to thrive.

Keep experimenting with ratios until the process feels effortless — every household's scrap mix is a little different, and the best compost recipe is the one you'll actually keep up with.
