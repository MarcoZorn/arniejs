Composting for Small Gardens

calculating… ·

Composting turns kitchen scraps and yard trimmings into rich, dark soil that feeds next season's beds. It sounds like alchemy, but it is really just controlled decay: microbes, moisture, and time working together in a pile you tend by hand.

Start with a mix of greens and browns. Greens are your nitrogen-rich scraps — vegetable peels, coffee grounds, fresh grass clippings. Browns are carbon-rich material — dry leaves, cardboard, straw. A rough ratio of two or three parts brown to one part green keeps the pile from turning slimy or smelling sour.

Turn the pile every week or two. This brings oxygen into the center, which keeps the right microbes thriving and speeds everything along. In warm weather, a well-tended pile can finish in as little as six to eight weeks. In cooler months, expect it to take longer, and that is perfectly fine — the ground is patient.

You will know it is ready when it looks, smells, and crumbles like fresh forest floor: dark, earthy, and free of recognizable scraps. Sift out anything that has not broken down yet and toss it back into a new pile to keep working.

A small backyard bin is enough to noticeably cut down on kitchen waste while building a steady supply of free, living soil amendment — one of the simplest trades a garden ever offers back.
