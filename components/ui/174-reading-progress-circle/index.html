Preparing your beds for first frost

As nights get colder, the work in the garden shifts from growing to protecting. This piece walks through mulching, covering, and the harvesting order that saves the most from an early frost.

Watch the forecast, not the calendar

Frost dates are averages, not guarantees. Track nightly lows once they start dipping below 5°C and keep row covers within reach.

A single unexpected cold snap can undo weeks of late-season growth if you're not ready to throw a cover over tender crops on short notice.

Mulch before the ground freezes

A thick layer of straw or shredded leaves insulates roots and slows the freeze-thaw cycles that heave perennials out of the soil.

Apply mulch after the first light frost has knocked back top growth but before the ground itself is frozen solid.

Harvest in the right order

Tender crops like basil and tomatoes go first — they're damaged by even a light frost. Root vegetables can often stay in the ground longer, sometimes improving in flavor after a cold snap.

Leafy brassicas like kale and collards actually sweeten with frost exposure, so there's no rush to pull those early.

Protect containers separately

Potted plants freeze faster than in-ground beds because roots are exposed on all sides. Move what you can indoors or against a sheltered wall.

For anything too heavy to move, wrapping the pot itself in burlap or bubble wrap adds meaningful insulation.

Plan for next season

Before you close up for winter, jot down what worked and what didn't. A five-minute note now saves guesswork when spring planning starts.
