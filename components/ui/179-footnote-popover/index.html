No-till growing preserves the fungal networks that trade sugars for minerals with plant roots, and over several seasons it can raise organic matter enough to change how a bed drains after heavy rain. The tradeoff is patience: undisturbed soil structure takes years to build and only minutes of tilling to undo.

Mulching heavily in the off season protects that structure through winter freeze-thaw cycles, and a two-inch layer of straw or leaf mold does most of the work on its own.

Mycorrhizal networks — fungal filaments that colonize roots and extend a plant's effective reach for water and phosphorus in exchange for photosynthetic sugars.

Organic matter & drainage — each 1% increase in soil organic matter can hold roughly 20,000 extra gallons of water per acre, improving both drought resilience and drainage.

Soil structure recovery — aggregate structure rebuilt by fungal hyphae and root channels can take three to five years to fully re-establish after a single deep till.

Winter mulch — a straw or leaf-mold layer buffers soil temperature swings, reducing frost heave that would otherwise fracture aggregate structure.
